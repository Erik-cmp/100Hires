> A common question about Zero Click Marketing: "Where do I do it?"
>
> Zero Click Marketing tends to work best in environments where people can get value without leaving the platform.
>
> In the newest episode of the podcast, I break it down into four:
>
> 1/ Social feeds (LinkedIn, TikTok, Instagram, X)
> 2/ Video platforms (YouTube, TikTok)
> 3/ Communities (Reddit, Slack, Discord)
> 4/ AI answers (ChatGPT, Claude)
>
> Different environments, same pattern:
>
> - native consumption
> - limited outbound links
> - influence happens before the click
>
> I also share a few examples, including a product launch from Seattle Ultrasonics that shows how a single piece of content can explain — and sell — an entirely new category.
>
> If you're trying to figure out where ZCM fits into your strategy, this episode will help.

## Post 2

- Source: [Search isn’t dead.](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_search-isnt-dead-its-so-alive-that-it-share-7445344362651152384-MpJn/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADeDmuEBWrqFxjlkKuiyfURJ38rOTztV0i4)

> Search isn’t dead.
>
> It’s so alive that it now happens across Google, Amazon, YouTube, Reddit, AI tools, and all kinds of other places people go to look for answers.
>
> Search is a behavior.
>
> A few recent receipts:
>
> - Rand Fishkin’s research shows Google is still absurdly massive. In his analysis of the top 5,000 most-visited websites, Google was as large as the next 13 sites combined. It explains why search so often gets credit for demand that was created somewhere else first.
> - Cyrus Shepard argues the job is not just to rank, or even just to win the click. It’s to look like the result most likely to satisfy the searcher — and then actually do it.
> - Ross Simmonds found that Reddit is showing up earlier in the buyer journey than many SaaS brands probably realize. In his research, buyers were often seeing Reddit discussions before vendor websites, even on general category searches.
> - Wil Reynolds learned the hard way that if your strongest proof points aren't published, AI and search systems may piece together weaker third-party evidence to fill the gap.
>
> This is a lot. And there's still much more.
